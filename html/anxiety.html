What is anxiety ?

In life, there are times when a person get anxiety over certain aspects of their life. Examples of these aspects are making a big decision in life, before taking am important test, or facing problems at work. However, anxiety disorders are more than just temporary worry or fear. For a person who is experiencing anxiety disorder, the anxiety does not simply go away and it could get worse as time goes on. The symptoms that the person experience can get in the way with their daily activities such as job performance, school work, or relationships.

Examples of anxiety disorders include generalized anxiety disorder, social anxiety disorder (social phobia), specific phobias and separation anxiety disorder. A person can have more than anxiety disorder.

With treatment, it can help a person who is experiencing anxiety.

Symptoms

Common anxiety signs and symptoms include:

  • Feeling nervous, restless or tense
  • Having a sense of impending danger, panic or doom
  • Having an increased heart rate
  • Breathing rapidly (hyperventilation)
  • Sweating
  • Trembling
  • Feeling weak or tired
  • Trouble concentrating or thinking about anything other than the present worry
  • Having trouble sleeping
  • Experiencing gastrointestinal (GI) problems
  • Having difficulty controlling worry
  • Having the urge to avoid things that trigger anxiety

Types of anxiety

The person is displayed as having excessive anxiety or worry for most of the days. This usually go on for at least 6 months. The persons worry could be about personal health, work, social interactions, and everyday routine life situations. The fear and anxiety that these people experience can result in them having a tough time in areas of their life, such as social interactions, school, and work.

Signs and symptoms:

  • Feeling restless, wound-up, or on-edge
  • Being easily fatigued
  • Having difficulty concentrating; mind going blank
  • Being irritable
  • Having muscle tension
  • Difficulty controlling feelings of worry
  • Having sleep problems, such as difficulty falling or staying asleep, restlessness, or unsatisfying sleep

The person is displayed to have reoccurring sudden panic attacks of uncontrollable fear that come on quickly and reach their peaks within minutes. These attacks can when the person least expect it to happen, or it could result from a trigger, such as a feared object or situation.

The person with panic disorder often worries about when the next panic attack will happen. To prevent this from happening, they will always try to avoid places, situation, or behaviours that can cause them to have panic attacks.

The worry about panic attacks and the effort spend trying to avoid these attacks from happening causes the person to have a hard time in various aspect of a person’s life, including the development of agoraphobia.

Signs and symptoms:

  • Heart palpitations, a pounding heartbeat, or an accelerated heartrate
  • Sweating
  • Trembling or shaking
  • Sensations of shortness of breath, smothering, or choking
  • Feelings of impending doom
  • Feelings of being out of control

People with agoraphobia have an intense fear of two or more of the following situations:

  • Using public transportation
  • Being in open spaces
  • Being in enclosed spaces
  • Standing in line or being in a crowd being outside of the home alone

People with agoraphobia usually avoid these types of situations because they think that it might be difficult or impossible to leave if they have panic-like reactions or other embarrassing symptoms. In certain extreme cases, the person will not to leave one’s house because to avoid the trouble of having to go through these situations.

A phobia is an extreme fear of certain object or situations. Although in certain situations, it can be realistic to be anxious, the fear that a person with phobias experience are out of proportion to the actual danger that the object or situation can cause.

People with a phobia:

  • May have an irrational or excessive worry about encountering the feared object or situation
  • Take active steps to avoid the feared object or situation
  • Experience immediate intense anxiety upon encountering the feared object or situation
  • Endure unavoidable objects and situations with intense anxiety

There are several types of phobias and phobia-related disorders:

Phobia type Description
Specific Phobias (simple phobias)

A person who have a specific phobia have an extreme fear, or feel intense fear about specific types of objects or situations. Some example of these specific phobias includes:

  • Flying
  • Heights
  • Specific animals, such as spiders, dogs, or snakes
  • Receiving injections
  • Blood
Social anxiety disorder (social phobia)

A person with social anxiety disorder has an extreme fear, or anxiety toward situation which involves social interaction or performance. They worry that actions or behaviours related with their anxiety will be judged negatively by others. This causes the person to feel embarrassed. This worry usually causes a person with social anxiety to avoid situations involving social interaction.

Social anxiety disorder can establish from a range of situations such as within the workplace or school environment.

Usually separation anxiety is associated with something that only children deal with. However, that is not always the case as adults can also be diagnosed with separation anxiety disorder.

A person who have separation anxiety disorder have fears being separated from the people whom they are attached to. They often worry that some sort of harm will happen to the person they are attached to. This fear prevents them from being separated with the person they are attached to and to avoid from being alone.

A person with separation anxiety might have nightmares about being separated from the people that they are attached to or experience physical symptoms when separation occurs, or is anticipated.

Selective mutism occurs when the person fails to speak in a specific social situation even though they have normal language skill. Selective mutism usually occurs before the age of 5 and is often associated with extreme shyness, fear of social embarrassment, compulsive traits, withdrawal, clinging behaviour, and temper tantrums. A person diagnosed with selective mutism are usually also diagnosed with other anxiety disorders.

Causes

Researchers are discovering that both genetic and environmental factors can contribute to the risk of developing an anxiety disorder. Although the causes for each type of anxiety disorder can vary, some general cause for all types of anxiety disorders include:

  • Temperamental traits of shyness or behavioural inhibition in childhood
  • Exposure to stressful and negative life or environmental events in early childhood or adulthood
  • A history of anxiety or other mental illnesses in biological relatives
  • Some physical health conditions, such as thyroid problems or heart arrhythmias, or caffeine or other substances/medications, can produce or aggravate anxiety symptoms; a physical health examination is helpful in the evaluation of a possible anxiety disorder

Treatment and therapies

Anxiety disorders are generally treated with psychotherapy, medication, or both. There are many ways to treat anxiety and people should work with their doctor to choose the treatment that is best for them.

  1. Psychotherapy

    2. Psychotherapy can help a person with anxiety disorders. In order to be effective, psychotherapy must be directed at the person’s specific type of anxieties and adjusted to suit his or her needs.

  2. Medication

    3. Medication can be used to help relieve symptoms experienced by a person diagnosed with anxiety disorder but cannot cure anxiety disorders. Medication for anxiety are prescribed by doctors, such as psychiatrist or primary care provider. The most common type of medications used for anxiety disorders are:

    • Anti-Anxiety Drugs
    • Antidepressants
    • Beta-Blockers
